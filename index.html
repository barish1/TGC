About Us

Designed by people who know the real challenges facing today’s teachers and students.

The authors of this curriculum are college professors with decades of experience in the classroom. A few years ago, we noticed a serious decline in students’ ability to talk out ideas and debate with each other in higher-level college classes. So we decided to work together to create a conversation-centered curriculum that works perfectly in introductory classes taught at universities, community colleges, and in dual enrollment classes.

Our goal is twofold. We want to give students the appropriate level of reading and critical engagement they need to challenge them and build essential skills for college. And we want to give teachers and schools a simple way to build consistency and rigor across introductory classes. This curriculum achieves both of those goals. We devised it for our own introductory classes and saw an immediate difference in student interest levels and learning outcomes.

We know that introductory classes can be challenging for teachers, administration, and, consequently, for students. We understand what it feels like to be a teacher assigned an introductory class at the last minute, with only a few days to pull together a teaching plan. We understand what it’s like to be an administrator who has three days to respond to a spike in enrollment that forces the creation of extra introductory classes before the start of term. This is why we created Great Conversations 101 as a ready-to-teach curriculum with scaffolded independent learning activities, research, Socratic discussion, and debate. The unique approach of this curriculum is that it is centered around great conversations that are central to the human experience. Who am I? What is justice? How do I know what truth is? Is it more important to be safe or free? And many more. It is designed to meet the learning objectives of any English, humanities, or communication class: research, reading, writing, rhetoric, and critical thinking skills. But it meets them through a learning plan that focuses on exploration and deep engagement with ideas and each other, rather than throw-away assessments. This makes the coursework more personal, compelling, and impactful for students and for teachers. How Do We Know What Truth Is?

This unit is available with a plan

Plato’s Allegory of the Cave asks students to think about the nature of truth. How do we know that what we see and believe is the truth? Have we ever tested the boundaries that limit our known reality? If we care about truth and knowledge, then what is required of us in order to seek it? Through group work, Socratic seminar discussion, debate, and a final project, students will examine these questions and engage with ancient and modern texts that disagree about whether our sense of reality has anything to do with truth.

Read Plato’s “Allegory of the Cave,” The Republic, Book VII.

Part One

Assign students to work in groups to discuss the following questions. This will help students prepare for a deeper discussion during the Socratic seminar in the second part of the curriculum.

  1. What do the prisoners see and hear in the cave, and what do they believe about the nature of their reality?
  2. How does Plato describe the experience of a person leaving the cave for the first time?
  3. What allegorical meaning does Socrates ascribe to the cave, the shadows and the world outside?
  4. Interpret Plato’s meaning in the following passage: “In the world of knowledge, the idea of good is seen last of all; and is seen only with an effort; and, when seen, is also inferred to be the universal author of all things beautiful and right, parent of light and of the lord of light in this visible world, and the immediate source of reason and truth in the intellectual; and that this is the power upon which he who would act rationally, either in public or private life must have his eye fixed.”
  5. Explain what Socrates means in the following quote: “The power and capacity of learning exists in the soul already; and that just as the eye was unable to turn from darkness to light without the whole body, so too the instrument of knowledge can only by the movement of the whole soul be turned from the world of becoming into that of being, and learn by degrees to endure the sight of being, and of the brightest and best of being, or in other words, of the good” (Plato 6)?
  6. Why does Plato believe those who have this kind of knowledge should be rulers?

(Optional) Group Project:

Read “Chapter One: Plato’s Doctrines” from the Stanford Encyclopedia of Philosophy. Explain the distinction Plato sees between “real” forms and “ideal” forms, or between the “visible” and the “intelligible.” Based on what you have read and what you can infer, what kind of education does Plato believe one needs in order to grasp the ideal forms of things?

Part Two

Arrange the desks in a circle and lead a Socratic discussion with the following prompts and questions.

  1. The shadows in the cave are deceptive imitations of reality that are accepted as truth out of habit and ease. The deception is reinforced through a social contract that gains credence by virtue of its being believed by the majority, not through inquiry, examination or a process of reasoning. With this allegory in mind, can you say with confidence that your own sense of reality is based on truth, rather than shadows? How do you know?
  2. Plato’s story examines the gulf between appearance and reality, or between truth and perception. Do you believe there is a difference between truth and perception? Or does our subjective perception create truth and reality for us?
  3. Plato suggests that a prisoner who sees the world outside the cave and returns to tell the other prisoners about it will not be believed. He writes: “Men would say of him that up he went and down he came without his eyes; and that it was better not even to think of ascending; and if any one tried to loose another and lead him up to the light, let them only catch the offender, and they would put him to death.”

Do you agree with the assertion that people in the cave would react this way? Why or why not? Have you ever experienced discomfort when faced with knowledge that contradicts your preconceived ideas of what is true?

  1. Is it possible to force people to see or know the truth? Why or why not?
  2. What is the aim of education, according to Plato? How does this compare to how we think about education in today’s society?
  3. Plato thinks that philosophers should be rulers. What is his rationale for this? What are Plato’s worries if someone other than a philosopher rules? What do you think? Would philosophers be the best kind of civic leaders and rulers?
  4. Many philosophers, including Plato, assert that the highest human pursuits are driven by a desire to know the truth about the world, about ourselves, about metaphysics, etc. Do you agree with this? If so, then how do you reconcile our truth-seeking nature with the fact that seeking the truth brings us discomfort?

(Optional) Before beginning the debate, read Ted Chiang’s “The Truth of Fact, The Truth of Feeling.”

Part Three

Choose one or more of these debate questions to pose to students. Ask each student to decide on their answer to the question(s) and to map out a few logical supporting premises. Next, ask them to consider what an opposing view to theirs would be and how they would rebut it. Finally, let students either pair up with an opponent to debate in person or combine students into teams to perform one class-wide debate.

  1. Is it better to be comfortable with illusion or uncomfortable with truth? Use examples from modern life to support your argument.
  2. Plato and Socrates speak of truth as a singular, constant ideal. They believed truth was objective and eternal. Protagoras (490-420 BCE) and other Sophists believed that truth is relative and subjective, and thus different for each individual person. Do you believe truth is relative or objective? Explain your answer. Spend some time researching the Sophists to prepare your argument.
  3. Do you agree that a democratic society should be ruled by people with the kind of knowledge that Plato describes? Why or why not?
  4. Should a society force people, through law and institutions, to achieve the higher order of knowledge that Plato describes? Why or why not?
  5. (Optional) Plato and Chiang present competing obstacles to knowing the truth: lack of information vs. the human desire to protect our existing beliefs. Which do you think is the greater obstacle? Explain your answer.
  6. (Optional) Does humanity control our technology or does technology reshape and control what we consider truth and reality?
  7. (Optional) Does our natural inclination for storytelling in the form of cultural stories and personal memories impede our quest for truth? Or does storytelling make truth? Explain your answer.

Give students as much time as they need in the classroom to respond to the prompt of their choice. The supplemental reading can be done for homework, but the writing should be done in the classroom.

  1. Compare and Contrast. Ask students to read “Learning to Read and Write,” a chapter from Narrative of the Life of Frederick Douglass as a comparative text.

What similarities and differences do you notice in the way the two authors treat ignorance, knowledge and truth?

  1. Argument. Read “Meditations 1 &2,” by René Descartes.

In an argument essay that considers both philosophers’ treatments of deception and reality, explain whether or not it is possible to escape illusion and know the truth. Set up a clear claim, support it with logic and evidence, consider an opposing viewpoint, and then rebut that opposing viewpoint to end your argument on a strong note.

  1. Analysis. Examine Ted Chiang’s understanding of what constitutes truth and reality. What relevance do facts have in truth-seeking? What else is needed to really grasp the truth? To what extent does technology reveal and/or impede the truth? Finally, compare Plato and Chiang’s conceptions of truth and their different interpretations of what we must do to find it.

Plato (428/427–348/347 B.C.E.) was an ancient Greek philosopher who wrote his ideas through dialogue, often using his teacher, Socrates, as his mouthpiece.

In Plato’s book The Republic, he examines political science and moral philosophy, which aims to answer the question, “How should one live?” “Allegory of the Cave” is a brief dialogue within that book that illustrates some of Plato’s ideas about education, democratic leadership and the distinction between appearance and reality.

The dialogue is between Socrates and Glaucon, Plato’s brother, and it begins with a description of a cave in which people have been chained since childhood. These people know nothing but the shadows and echoes that comprise their reality in the cave. Socrates asks Glaucon to imagine what would happen if one of those people managed to leave the cave and step out into the sunlight. He describes the initial discomfort the person would experience, followed by revelation and deep learning of the world beyond the shadows that they had always known. Next, Socrates asks Glaucon to imagine how the other people in the cave would react if this person returned to them and explained what he saw.

This allegory helps us understand education as our own liberation from the chains of unexamined assumptions about what truth really is. It is a difficult and, as Socrates describes it, a truly uncomfortable process of training our eyes to see anew in the light. The dialogue ends with a discussion of the responsibility that comes with a transformative education.

The pursuit of truth is central to Plato’s vision of a valuable education, and such a pursuit requires inquiry, courage, and a willingness to push through the pain of searing light in our eyes. Plato believed that those few who really fought hard enough to liberate themselves from chains and shadows would experience the rewards of knowing the “ideal forms of things.” The ideal form of a sunflower, for example, is the perfect model all the sunflowers strive to imitate. The same is true for love. Goodness. Justice. The versions we know in the “visible” world are just pale imitations of the ideal forms of these notions. It takes a mind that can perceive the truth of things beyond appearances to really understand what the ideal form of justice, goodness and love really are.

Frederick Douglass was a writer and statesman who was born into slavery in the nineteenth century. He examines the ideas central to Plato’s writing in a chapter of his memoir titled, “Learning to Read and Write,” in which he treats his education as a path toward freedom and truth. As he learns to read, he begins to understand the injustice of slavery and the forces designed to keep him powerless. His awakening closely resembles the prisoner’s journey in Plato’s “Allegory of the Cave,” in which a person escapes a world of shadows and gradually sees reality more clearly. For both Douglass and Plato, seeking truth is difficult and even painful, because knowledge destroys comforting illusions. Yet once the truth is seen, it becomes impossible to return willingly to ignorance.

Two optional additional readings are available in this unit: Ted Chiang’s short story, “The Truth of Fact, The Truth of Feeling,” and René Descartes’ “Meditations 1&2.” They both take different approaches to exploring the difficulty of knowing whether our beliefs correspond to reality. While Plato’s prisoners mistake shadows for truth because those shadows are the only world they have ever known, Descartes questions whether the senses, dreams, or even an evil deceiver might make all human experience unreliable. Chiang brings this problem into the modern world by showing how memory, technology, and recorded evidence can challenge the stories people tell themselves about the past and about who they are. He raises questions about where in a matrix of facts, subjectivity, history, memory and increasingly powerful technology does the truth actually exist?

Together, Plato, Descartes and Chiang suggest that false beliefs arise from many sources: ignorance; unreliable perception, imperfect memory, our relationship to technology, and the human desire to preserve a comfortable understanding of reality. Each text therefore asks whether truth can be reached simply by gaining more information, or whether genuine knowledge also requires the willingness to question the assumptions through which we interpret the world.

What is Justice?

This unit raises questions about how students define justice and what the responsibility of the individual is when law and justice don’t align. The following questions take students through independent group work, a Socratic seminar, debate, and several options for writing assignments. The class activities cross reference philosophical and political texts from the ancient world and the modern one, to help students think about how we navigate our moral obligations to family, to manmade laws, to higher or divine laws, to the self, and to society.

Antigone, by Sophocles

Part One — Group Work

Assign students to work in groups to discuss the following questions. This will help them prepare for the discussion and debate that follow.

  1. Why does Creon refuse to allow Polyneices to be buried?
  2. Why does Antigone decide to bury Polyneices even though she knows the punishment? What is the importance of the burial ritual to her?
  3. Why does Ismene refuse to help Antigone bury their brother? What do you make of her statement that Antigone has “lost her mind” but holds to “the love of her loved ones” in lines 98-99?
  4. What is the narrative function of the chorus? How do they influence your understanding of the other dramatic elements in the play, such as character, conflict and setting?
  5. What persuasive appeal does Haemon make to his father on behalf of Antigone? How does his appeal to his father differ from Antigone’s presentation of her case to Creon?
  6. Does Antigone have the power to save herself from a death sentence? Why does she choose to accept Creon’s punishment?
  7. Antigone and Creon are each driven by principle and tragic flaw. What are Creon’s principles? What are Antigone’s principles? What is Creon’s tragic flaw? What is Antigone’s tragic flaw?
  8. What makes the ending of this play so tragic, in your opinion?

Arrange the desks in a circle and lead a Socratic discussion with the following prompts and questions.

  1. What is the difference between law and justice? Do you believe justice would have been served if Creon had allowed Polyneices to have a burial ceremony? Why or why not? What moral authority has the most influence on your answer?
  2. To what extent does perspective control how we see justice and injustice? If you answer that perspective does influence our sense of justice, then how can we know that our actions are truly, objectively just?
  3. In lines 84-97, Sophocles reveals Antigone’s sense of obligation to a higher law than Creon’s. Examine what these lines reveal about Antigone’s character and her sense of moral duty. Examine what they reveal about Ismene’s sense of moral duty. Do you believe one of these characters has a greater grasp of justice than the other? Explain your answer.
  4. If you had fought in “The War of the Seven Against Thebes,” do you think you would be more sympathetic to Creon’s view of justice or Antigone’s? Explain your answer and how your sense of justice influences it.
  5. If you were in Ismene’s position, do you think you would have reacted to Antigone or Creon differently than Ismene did? Explain your answer and how your sense of justice influences it.
  6. In ancient Greek, there are a few different words used to describe a king. One is “tyrannus,” which means an absolute ruler unbound by law or constitution. We derive the word tyrant from this root. Another common term for king or ruler was “basileus,” which describes the title of king without the implication of despotism. Do you think Sophocles’ audience would have used the word “tyrannus” or “basileus” to describe Creon? Why?
  7. On line 636, Haemon tells Creon, “Give me good advice and I will follow it.” Analyze what Haemon is asking of his father in this statement and what it reveals about justice as both a personal virtue and one rooted in inter-generational cultural values. Was Creon able to give Haemon what he wanted? In what way did the ensuing conversation affect the fates of both of these men? Do you believe either man had a stronger claim to having justice on his side in the exchange between them?
  8. Creon and Antigone would both describe themselves as obedient to the moral laws that they deem supreme. Have you ever found yourself in a conflict where you and the person with whom you disagreed both believed you held the moral high ground? How did that conviction make you behave and feel about the other person? Do you take a different view of that conflict after reading Antigone?
  9. Aristotle describes tragedy as an art form that elicits “fear and pity” from the audience. The pity is for the tragic characters consigned to a cruel fate that they could have avoided, and the fear is rooted in our sense that their fate could be our own. Examine what and who in this play makes you feel fear and pity. Explain your answer.
  10. On line 525, Creon says, “As long as I live, I will not be ruled by a woman.” What does he mean when he says this to Antigone? What role does gender play in the power dynamics of this play? To what extent does power determine what justice is? To what extent do we perceive justice relative to our own relationship to power? Do you believe that power is inherently unjust? Or that the powerless are inherently just? Explain your answer.

Further Writing on Justice:

A philosopher named John Rawls invented the following thought experiment at Harvard: Imagine that you must design a fair society, but you do not know who you will be in that society. You don’t know what you will look like, what your talents will be, whether you will be rich or poor, whether or not you will be healthy, etc. Without knowing yourself, you will be without self-interest or bias in the task of designing a fair society. He called this thought experiment “The Original Position.” He believed designing a society from behind “the veil of ignorance” was the most effective way to figure out what the most just possible society would really look like.

Read about John Rawls and “The Original Position.” Then put on the veil of ignorance and design the most just society you can imagine without knowing who you will be in it. Describe that society. What makes it just?

Should People Pursue Justice?

Selections from: On the Commonwealth

By Marcus Tullius Cicero

(Written in Rome between 54 and 51 B.C.—Discovered in fragments in the 19th century)

XI. Philus: [If justice were natural, then nature] would have laid down our laws; all peoples would be subject to the same laws; and the same people would not be subject to different laws at different times. Now, I put the question to you: If it be the duty of a just man and a good citizen to obey the laws, what laws should he obey? Shall he obey any laws that happen to prevail? But surely rectitude does admit of inconsistency, and nature does not permit different standards of conduct. Laws, therefore, are obeyed because of the penalties they may inflict and not because of our sense of justice. Consequently, the law has no sanction in nature.

It follows, then, that humans are not just by nature. Or, can we believe that, while there is diversity in styles of human legislation, good people follow true justice rather than that which is merely thought to be just?

XII: If a man wishes to act in accordance with justice, and yet is ignorant of the divine* law, he will honor the enactments of his own people as if they were the true law, although they are in general the product, not of justice, but of self-interest. Why indeed have diverse and unlike codes of law been established among all peoples? Is it not because each people has enacted for itself such provisions as it thought to be useful in its own conditions of life? But how wide is the divergence between justice and utility may be learned from the case of the Roman people itself.

For unless I am mistaken, every kingdom or empire is acquired by war and extended by victory, and furthermore, the chief element in war and victory is the capture and overthrow of cities. Such acts are impossible without doing injury to the gods. Hence, the irreligious acts of the Roman people equal the number of its trophies; every triumph over a people is a triumph over its gods; the collections of booty equal in number the surviving images of captive gods.

Therefore, men have established laws to serve their own advantages. These laws, of course, were different to suit the different characters of peoples, and even in the same people they were often modified to accord with changing conditions.

All human beings, as well as all other living creatures, are led by nature to consult their own self-interest. Hence, either there is no such thing as justice, or, if there is, it is the height of folly, since a person would do injury to himself by consulting the interests of others.

The Greek philosopher Carneades wrote: Ever to count your country’s welfare first. And what is a country? For all those who have the power of life and death over a people are tyrants, but they prefer to take the name which belongs to Jupiter the Most High and to call themselves kings. And, when a group of men controls the commonwealth by virtue of their wealth, their birth, or any advantages they happen to possess, they form an oligarchy, but they call themselves leading citizens. And, again, if the people have the supreme power and if all public business is carried on at their pleasure, we have what is called liberty (but what, in fact, is license).

Now, when the citizens fear each other, when man fears man and class fears class, no one feels secure; and, as a result, a contract, as we call it, is made between the commons and those with power. Thus, neither nature nor deliberate choice is the mother of justice. Weakness is the mother of justice. For since a man must choose one of three possibilities—either to act unjustly without suffering injustice, or both to act unjustly and suffer injustice, or neither to act unjustly nor suffer injustice—the best choice in each man’s self-interest is to act unjustly without suffering the consequences, if you can. The second choice is neither to act unjustly nor to suffer injustice. But, the most wretched condition of all is an incessant warfare in which men both do and suffer injustice.

XVII: I put the question to you: Let us suppose that there are two men, one of whom is thoroughly upright and honorable, a man of consummate justice and unique integrity, while the other is a man of extraordinary depravity and shamelessness. And let us assume that the state in which they live is so misguided as to believe the good man a monster of unspeakable criminality, while, on the other hand, it considers the scoundrel to be a model of uprightness and good faith. Let us suppose further that, in conformity with this error on the part of all the citizens, the good man is persecuted, harassed, has his hands cut off and his eyes gouged out, is condemned, cast into chains, tortured by fire, exiled, and reduced to destitution. Finally, let us assume he is universally regarded as justly meriting his wretched condition. On the other hand, let us suppose that the evil man is praised, honored, and esteemed by all; that all sorts of offices, civil and military, and every form of influence and wealth are conferred upon him; and then he is universally held to be an excellent man. Who under these circumstances will be so mad as to doubt which of the two lots he would prefer?

What is true of individuals is also true of states. There is no country so stupid as not to prefer unjust dominion to just subjection.

Carneades investigated the tension between a man’s desire to be just and his instinct to save his own life. For he said: Certainly justice requires us not to kill human beings and not in any way to touch another’s property. What, then, will the just man do if he is in a shipwreck and someone weaker than himself has found a plank [on which to keep afloat]? Will he not push the weaker man off the plank, that he may get on it himself and thus make his escape? Especially when there is no one in the middle of the sea to bear witness against him? If he is prudent, he will do this, for he will inevitably lose his own life if he does not. If, however, he would rather die than raise his hand against the other man, he is indeed just, but he is a fool, because in sparing another man’s life he fails to spare his own.

Similarly, does not a nation under attack act in the interest of its country, first?

Thus the difference between justice and prudence. Prudence saves the life of the man at sea, and justice forfeits it. Which of these qualities is truly inborn in human nature?

Laelius: There is in fact a true law—namely, right reason—which is in accordance with nature, applies to all men, and is unchangeable and eternal. By its commands this law summons men to the performance of their duties; by its prohibitions it restrains them from doing wrong. Its commands and prohibitions always influence good men, but are without effect upon the bad. Sophocles’ Antigone is one of the most important surviving works of ancient Greek tragedy. First performed in Athens in the fifth century BCE, the play belongs to a culture in which drama explored important issues in civic, religious, and political life. Greek tragedies were performed at major festivals, where citizens gathered to watch stories about gods, heroes, families, war, justice, and the limits of human power. These plays allowed Athenians to think publicly about difficult moral questions facing both individuals and the city.

Antigone takes place after a civil war in Thebes. Two brothers, Eteocles and Polyneices, have killed each other fighting for control of the city. Their uncle Creon, now king, declares that Eteocles will receive an honorable burial, while Polyneices, whom Creon considers a traitor, must remain unburied. In ancient Greek culture, burial was a sacred duty owed to the dead and to the gods. Antigone, sister of the two dead brothers, decides to bury Polyneices despite Creon’s law. Her act sets the central conflict of the play in motion.

The play is often read as a conflict between divine law and human law, or between conscience and political authority. Antigone believes that some duties are older and higher than the commands of any ruler. Creon believes that the survival of the city depends on obedience to law, especially after civil war. Both characters are unyielding in their convictions. Antigone is determined and supposes the righteousness of divine law to be on her side. Creon begins with a reasonable concern for public order, but becomes increasingly rigid, proud, and unable to listen.

A major idea in the play is the Greek concept of hubris, often translated as excessive pride or overstepping human limits. Creon’s tragedy comes from his refusal to recognize that political power has limits. He treats his own decree as absolute and dismisses the claims of family, religion, public opinion, and tradition. By the time he realizes his mistake, the damage cannot be undone. Antigone’s tragedy is borne of a combination of fate and character. She is courageous and moral for challenging Creon, but her convictions make her righteous and inflexible, which dooms her to an abandonment of reason and, ultimately, a tragic end. The themes in this play range from the personal themes of independence of mind, cultural themes about the importance of rituals and traditions, and political themes of civil disobedience and the conflict between individual conscience and the edict of the state.

Antigone is a tragedy, which Aristotle defined as a form of theater that evokes fear and pity from its audience, and in which the hero of the story experiences a tragic downfall due to a fatal character flaw. Creon’s refusal to hear a contradictory view is one of his fatal flaws. At one point, the chorus accuses Antigone of being “impetuous,” pointing to one of the fatal flaws in her character. The crux of a tragedy is the haunting truth that the outcome could have been avoided. If only Creon had listened, or if Antigone had reasoned without passion, both tragic endings could have been prevented.

One reason why Greek tragedy is timeless is because it captures the reality of human moral drama. In Antigone, it is easy to see validity in Antigone’s position and in Creon’s. There is no good vs. evil construct in the play. They are just two flawed characters with easily understood motives who become embroiled in their own tragic undoing. The audience experiences the frustration of a rational observer who sees opportunity for compromise or reason that the emotion and conviction of each character eclipses from their sight.

The tragic tale of Antigone asks what happens when two deeply held principles collide: loyalty to the city and loyalty to family; obedience to law and obedience to conscience; order and justice; compromise and moral certainty. Its lasting power comes from the fact that these conflicts still feel alive. When should a person obey the law? When is it right to resist? Can a society survive without order? Can it be just if its laws violate basic human duties?

For students today, Antigone offers a way to think about the relationship between the individual and the state. It asks whether law is always the same thing as justice, whether moral responsibility can require disobedience, and whether power becomes dangerous when it refuses to listen. More than two thousand years after it was first performed, the play remains central because it dramatizes a problem every society must face: what should a person do when the law says one thing, but conscience says another?

This unit offers students connective texts to broaden, deepen and modernize the consideration of justice. Martin Luther King’s “Letter from a Birmingham Jail” addresses the same conflict between individual conscience and state law during the Civil Rights Movement. Alexis de Tocqueville’s excerpt from Democracy in America, written in 1835, considers the conflict between the individual and the tyranny of the majority. In “Dead Men’s Path,” Chinua Achebe examines the conflict between obligation to state law vs. obligation to a higher law in Nigeria in 1949, during the final stretch of English colonial rule. John Rawls, a twentieth-century Harvard philosopher, weighs the real considerations of justice in his thought experiment called “The Original Position,” which challenges students to adopt something called a “veil of ignorance” to imagine a fair society without knowing who they will be in that society. Finally, an excerpt from Cicero questions if justice is really inherent to the human condition.

By the end of this unit, students will have had opportunities to think about, discuss and debate justice in ways that will challenge them to add complexity, nuance and deeper understanding to the way they think about justice and the pursuit of it in their own lives.

Virgil's Aeneid, Book II

Written by Virgil close to the year 30 BCE, the Aeneid was commissioned during the reign of Augustus to tell the legendary story of Rome's origins. Augustus commissioned Virgil to write this tale, because he wanted the poet’s established thematic focus on hard work, sensibility and religion to reclaim order in an empire that had just experienced civil war.

The poem follows the Trojan hero Aeneas, who escapes the destruction of Troy and undertakes a long journey that will ultimately lead to the founding of the Roman people. Modeled after the epic traditions of Homer, the Aeneid explores themes of duty, sacrifice, leadership, and the tension between personal desires and obligations to a greater purpose.

Book II is one of the most dramatic sections of the epic. Speaking as a guest in the court of Queen Dido, Aeneas recounts the final night of Troy. He tells how the Greeks deceived the Trojans with the famous wooden horse, how the city was invaded under cover of darkness, and how Troy was consumed by fire and violence. Amid the chaos, Aeneas struggles to protect his family while coming to terms with the realization that his homeland cannot be saved. Rather than dying with his city, he accepts the painful responsibility of preserving its future by leading the survivors into exile.

As you read, consider how Virgil presents leadership during times of catastrophe. Why does Aeneas choose survival over a heroic last stand? How does the fall of Troy become the beginning of something new? How is his sense of duty divided? Book II invites readers to reflect on the costs of leadership, the responsibilities individuals owe to past and future generations, and the ways societies preserve hope even in moments of devastating loss.

Bhagavad Gita, Chapters 1–2

The Bhagavad Gita ("The Song of the Lord") forms part of the larger Mahabharata, one of the world's longest and most influential epic poems. Although the Mahabharata recounts a vast conflict between rival branches of a royal family, the Bhagavad Gita narrows its focus to a single moment immediately before battle begins. Standing between two massive armies, the warrior prince Arjuna realizes that he must fight against relatives, teachers, and lifelong friends. Overcome with grief and moral uncertainty, he lowers his bow and questions whether any victory could justify such a terrible cost.

Chapters 1 and 2 introduce the central philosophical conversation of the Gita. Arjuna's charioteer, Krishna, who is ultimately revealed to be a divine figure, responds by challenging Arjuna's understanding of duty, justice, life, and death. Krishna argues that individuals must fulfill their moral responsibilities without becoming attached to personal gain or fear of loss. The dialogue explores ideas of courage, selfless action, and the relationship between one's individual conscience and a larger moral order.

Like Aeneas in the Aeneid, Arjuna faces a profound conflict between personal emotion and public duty. Both works ask what leaders owe to others when confronted with impossible choices. Yet the two epics offer different answers rooted in their respective cultural and religious traditions.

Context for What Is Happiness

For thousands of years, philosophers have asked one of humanity's oldest and most enduring questions: What does it mean to live a happy life? While happiness is often understood today as a feeling of pleasure or personal satisfaction, the thinkers in this unit challenge us to consider whether happiness is something deeper—something connected to virtue, moral responsibility, justice, or the well-being of others.

In Nicomachean Ethics, one of the foundational works of Western philosophy. Aristotle argues that true happiness, or eudaimonia, is the result of living a life of virtue, reason, and moral excellence. According to Aristotle, happiness is not a passing emotion or the product of material achievement, like wealth or fame. Those things are nice, but they don’t have any value on his happiness index. Instead, Aristotle values the development of good character, the life of the mind and the soul, and the fulfillment of one’s potential. This is why the word he uses to describe happiness, eudaimonia, is often translated to mean “flourishing.”

More than two thousand years later, the English philosophers Jeremy Bentham and John Stuart Mill proposed a very different answer. Their philosophy, known as utilitarianism, argues that the moral value of an action should be judged by its consequences. Bentham maintained that the best action is the one that produces the greatest happiness for the greatest number of people, treating pleasure and pain as the ultimate measures of morality. Mill agreed with Bentham's basic principle, but he argued that not all pleasures are equal. Intellectual, artistic, and moral pursuits, he claimed, represent "higher pleasures" that contribute more fully to human happiness than simple physical enjoyment. Finally, Ursula LeGuin’s short story, “The Ones Who Walk Away from Omelas,” puts the philosophy of utilitarianism to the ultimate test, questioning whether or not happiness is real if it relies on a deliberate injustice.

Taken together, these readings trace the evolution of one of philosophy's central debates. Is happiness primarily about pleasure, personal virtue, collective well-being, or something else entirely? Can happiness be measured? Are some forms of happiness more valuable than others? And what happens when the pursuit of happiness conflicts with justice, compassion, or individual rights? By comparing these texts, students will examine competing visions of the good life and consider what responsibilities we bear to ourselves and others.

Who Am I?

Many of history's greatest thinkers have argued that progress often begins with individuals who are willing to question accepted beliefs. This lesson explores one of the central questions of philosophy and democratic life: What responsibilities do individuals have to think for themselves, and what obligations do they owe to society?

The first reading, Plato's Apology, presents the defense speech of Socrates before an Athenian jury in 399 BCE. Charged with corrupting the youth and introducing new gods, Socrates refuses to abandon his lifelong commitment to questioning assumptions and examining accepted beliefs. Rather than pleading for his life, he argues that the pursuit of truth and self-examination are essential to both individual character and the health of a free society. His declaration that "the unexamined life is not worth living" has become one of the defining statements of Western philosophy, challenging readers to consider whether loyalty to truth should outweigh loyalty to public opinion.

More than two thousand years later, W.E.B. DuBois and Simone De Beauvoir add to the conversation about free thinking and individuality by pointing out that inequality in a society denies many the privilege of developing a fully autonomous self to examine. W.E.B. DuBois discusses “double-consciousness” as an idea of seeing and understanding your own identity through the eyes of others. Simone de Beauvoir, who is only referenced in the Socratic seminar, discusses a similar idea in the concept of female identity existing in the “male gaze.” Students will be asked to think about what challenges Socrates might not have considered to becoming one’s authentic self before living a well-examined life.

In “Self-reliance,” American essayist Ralph Waldo Emerson tackles nonconformity. Writing during the nineteenth century, Emerson argued that individuals should trust their own reason, intuition, and conscience rather than conform to the expectations of society. He believed that creativity, originality, and moral courage arise only when individuals are willing to resist the pressure to imitate others. Students will have an opportunity to decide for themselves just how far Emerson’s advice can be taken without creating chaos.

The Danish philosopher Søren Kierkegaard approached the problem from a different perspective in The Present Age. Observing the growth of mass politics, newspapers, and public opinion, Kierkegaard warned that modern society encourages people to hide within what he famously called "the crowd." Rather than taking personal responsibility for their beliefs and actions, individuals often surrender their judgment to popular opinion or social approval. Kierkegaard argues that authentic living requires the courage to stand apart from the crowd and accept responsibility for one's own choices.

Although these writers lived in different cultures and eras, they share a common concern: the tension between independent thought and social conformity. Each asks whether truth can be discovered by following the majority, whether individuals have a duty to question accepted beliefs, and what sacrifices may be required of those who refuse to conform. At the same time, they differ in their understanding of how independent thinking should be grounded and to what extent and in what way one’s society is in conflict with one’s individuality.

Throughout history, people have looked to government for protection from violence, injustice, and disorder. Yet every society must also decide how much individual freedom its citizens are willing to surrender in exchange for that protection. This tension between security and liberty lies at the heart of modern political philosophy and remains one of the defining questions of democratic life. How much power should governments possess? When does authority become tyranny? Can a society be both safe and free?

This lesson examines these questions through the writings of two of the most influential political philosophers of the modern era: Thomas Hobbes and Jean-Jacques Rousseau. Although both thinkers believed that governments arise through a social contract, an agreement among individuals to form a political community, they reached dramatically different conclusions about the relationship between freedom and political authority.

Writing during the turmoil of the English Civil War, Thomas Hobbes believed that life without government would descend into fear, violence, and perpetual conflict. In Leviathan, he describes the "state of nature" as a condition in which every person must constantly struggle for survival. Because no common authority exists to enforce laws or settle disputes, life becomes insecure and unpredictable. Hobbes argues that rational people would willingly surrender many of their freedoms to a powerful government, because peace and security are impossible without a strong authority capable of maintaining order. For Hobbes, safety is the foundation upon which all other human pursuits depend.

Nearly a century later, Jean-Jacques Rousseau challenged this conclusion. In The Social Contract, Rousseau argues that people are born free and that legitimate governments derive their authority from the consent of the governed, not from force. Rather than viewing liberty and political order as opposing goals, Rousseau believes that citizens can remain free while participating in a government that reflects the collective will of the people. True political authority, he argues, does not require individuals to surrender their freedom but instead enables them to protect it through laws they help create. But there are dangers to Rousseau’s political vision, as well. Students will think about what happens to individuals who disagree with Rousseau’s conception of the “General Will.”

Together, Hobbes and Rousseau present two enduring visions of political life. Hobbes warns that excessive freedom can lead to chaos and insecurity. Rousseau warns that excessive authority can destroy the very freedom government is meant to protect. Their disagreement continues to shape debates over civil liberties, national security, public health, criminal justice, surveillance, and the limits of governmental power.

Between Hobbes and Rousseau stands the English philosopher John Locke, whose ideas helped shape many modern democratic governments. While Locke agreed with Hobbes that governments arise through a social contract, he rejected the belief that citizens must surrender broad authority to a powerful ruler. Instead, Locke argued that all people possess natural rights, including life, liberty, and property, that exist before government itself. The primary purpose of government, he believed, is to protect these rights, and when it fails to do so, citizens have the right to alter or even replace it. Locke's philosophy became a cornerstone of liberal democracy and profoundly influenced the authors of the United States Declaration of Independence, who adopted his conviction that legitimate governments derive "their just powers from the consent of the governed."

As you read these texts, and The Declaration of Independence at the end of the unit, consider how their ideas continue to influence contemporary society. In times of crisis, should governments receive greater authority to protect the public? Should individual freedoms ever be limited for the common good? And where should a free society draw the line between security and liberty?

Beauty has inspired some of humanity's greatest works of art, literature, music, and architecture. It has launched wars, inspired scientific discovery, shaped religious devotion, and transformed the way people understand themselves and the world around them. Yet despite its influence, beauty remains one of philosophy's most difficult questions. Is beauty simply a matter of personal taste, or does it exist independently of our opinions? Is it found only in works of art and nature, or can it also be discovered in virtue, truth, and the human soul? Does beauty have a moral purpose, or is it valuable simply because it exists?

This lesson explores these questions through influential voices spanning more than two thousand years of human thought. Together, they reveal that beauty is a window into some of philosophy's deepest questions about reality, goodness, and what it means to be human.

The ancient Roman/Syrian/Greek critic Longinus asks why certain literature, speeches, and works of art leave us awestruck. Great beauty, he argues, first pleases the senses, and then it elevates the human spirit, overwhelms ordinary experience, and inspires feelings of wonder. His distinction between the beautiful and the sublime would influence artists, writers, and philosophers for centuries.

A different view is examined in Plato's Symposium, where beauty is presented in human form as well as in art. Through the famous speech of Socrates on Diotima, Plato argues that love of beautiful things can lead us toward increasingly higher forms of understanding, from admiration of individual beauty to appreciation of beautiful souls, just laws, knowledge, and ultimately Beauty itself, an eternal and unchanging reality. For Plato, beauty is a path to wisdom.

Across the world in ancient China, Lao Tzu offers another understanding of beauty in the Tao Te Ching. Rather than treating beauty as an ideal to be pursued, Lao Tzu suggests that beauty and ugliness, strength and weakness, success and failure all exist in relationship to one another. Perfection and ideal forms don’t matter nearly as much as harmony, which aligns with virtues of balance, humility, and living in accordance with the Tao, or "the Way." His philosophy invites readers to see beauty as something revealed through the natural order of the world, rather than something to possess.

Shakespeare offers a more personal and human perspective in Sonnet 130. Rejecting the exaggerated praise common in Renaissance love poetry, Shakespeare insists that genuine love does not depend upon impossible standards of physical perfection. By celebrating the beauty of an ordinary woman rather than an idealized fantasy, he suggests that authenticity may be more beautiful than perfection itself. Later, students will have a chance to examine views on love from another famous sonnet writer, Petrarch.

Oscar Wilde challenges nearly every assumption developed by the earlier writers. In the famous preface to The Picture of Dorian Gray, Wilde argues that art does not exist to teach morality or improve society. Instead, he defends the idea that beauty possesses value in its own right. His philosophy of "art for art's sake" raises a provocative question that remains central to modern aesthetics: Must beauty serve truth or goodness, or is beauty itself enough? Finally, another humorous writer, Jonathan Swift, weighs in on the subject with “The Lady’s Dressing Room,” a satire that both critiques and affirms lofty views of beauty, while poking fun at the absurdity of idealizing beauty.

Taken together, these readings reveal that beauty has never meant just one thing. For Plato, beauty leads us toward truth. For Longinus, it elevates the soul through awe. For Lao Tzu, it emerges through harmony and balance. For Shakespeare, it is found in honest human affection. For Wilde, beauty stands independent of moral purpose. By comparing these perspectives, students will discover that debates about beauty are ultimately debates about human nature and about what we value, what we pursue, and whether beauty has the power to change us.

Can this really be true? Can we be so fooled by our own systems of thought that we believe we are free and thinking our own thoughts when we aren’t? Explain your answer and discuss whether or not you find Nietzsche’s writing persuasive.

Context for Unit 10

Human beings have long believed that they possess the freedom to choose their own actions and shape their own destinies. Yet philosophers, writers, and scientists have repeatedly questioned whether our choices are truly our own. Are we free to determine the course of our lives, or are our decisions shaped by forces beyond our control? What power does fate, divine providence, culture, language, technology, or history hold over us? This unit explores one of philosophy's oldest and most enduring questions: Do we have free will?

The conversation begins with Plato's Myth of Er, where souls choose the lives they will live before returning to earth, suggesting that freedom carries profound moral responsibility. Boethius' Consolation of Philosophy introduces another challenge by asking how human freedom can exist if divine providence possesses perfect knowledge of the future. An informal examination of “linguistic determinism” invites students to consider whether the language we speak influences the very way we perceive reality and the choices we are capable of imagining. Martin Heidegger's The Question Concerning Technology argues that technology fundamentally shapes how we understand ourselves and the world by “enframing” meaning, importance and possibilities of the phenomena we encounter. Friedrich Nietzsche's Beyond Good and Evil challenges readers to question whether their beliefs, values and choices are genuinely their own or simply inherited from culture and tradition, while his thought experiment of the "Eternal Recurrence" asks us to imagine whether we would freely choose to live our lives exactly as they are, again and again, forever.

James Baldwin's “My Dungeon Shook” brings these philosophical questions into the realities of history and society. Writing during the Civil Rights Movement, Baldwin reflects on the ways race, prejudice, and inherited social structures shape individual lives while insisting that people still bear the responsibility to choose who they become. Finally, students will recall Ovid's story of Phaethon, whose tragic attempt to control the sun's chariot raises timeless questions about ambition, self-mastery, and the consequences of choices made without wisdom.

Together, these readings suggest that freedom is far more complicated than simply choosing between alternatives. Our lives are shaped by powerful influences, including fate, language, technology, culture, and history; yet each author also asks whether genuine freedom remains possible despite these constraints. By comparing these perspectives, students will examine whether free will exists and what it truly means to live as a responsible human being.
